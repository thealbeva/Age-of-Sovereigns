# Player Classes

## Purpose

Player classes are long-term military identities.

They are not hard faction locks, and they do not prevent a player from training any troop type. Instead, each class changes how a player uses formations, which battlefield problems they solve best, and what role they naturally fill inside an alliance.

Core rule:

`Classes shape performance, not access.`

A Knight can train scouts. A Ranger can train heavy cavalry. A SellSword can operate siege engines. They simply do not receive the same class benefits as a specialist using those formations in their intended role.

The six player classes are:

- Knight
- Man-At-Arms
- Engineer
- Ranger
- Cavalry
- SellSword

The design goal is that every class answers a different war question:

- Knight: Who breaks the enemy's will?
- Man-At-Arms: Who holds the line?
- Engineer: Who breaks or preserves the wall?
- Ranger: Who knows what is really happening?
- Cavalry: Who controls movement and pursuit?
- SellSword: Who can afford to keep fighting?

## Class Summary

| Class | Primary Formations | Core Role | Best Phase |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Knight | Heavy Cavalry, Heavy Infantry | Shock and decisive commitment | Charge, breach follow-through |
| Man-At-Arms | Heavy Infantry, Light Infantry, Pikemen | Discipline and holding power | Line fighting, breach defense |
| Engineer | Battering Ram, Trebuchet, Catapult, walls, traps | Siege and fortification | Pre-breach, counter-siege |
| Ranger | Scouts, ShortBow, LongBow, CrossBow | Intelligence and ranged fieldcraft | Scouting, ambush, prepared defense |
| Cavalry | Ranged Cavalry, Light Cavalry, Heavy Cavalry | Mobility and pursuit | Open field, interception, routs |
| SellSword | Mixed formations, recovery systems | Cost control and flexible war logistics | Sustained campaigns |

## Knight

Knights are the doctrine of noble shock, heavy commitment, and battlefield prestige.

They are not simply "the cavalry class." Knights use elite formations to create decisive moments: the charge that breaks morale, the armored push through a breach, the counterattack that turns a battle.

### Formation Focus

- Heavy Cavalry
- Heavy Infantry

### Battlefield Identity

Knights are best when they commit at the correct moment and force the enemy to collapse.

They should feel powerful, expensive, proud, and dangerous to misuse.

Knight Heavy Cavalry is not the same as Cavalry Heavy Cavalry. Knight Heavy Cavalry is about impact and morale shock. Cavalry Heavy Cavalry is about mobility, pursuit, and controlling where the impact happens.

Knight Heavy Infantry is not the same as Man-At-Arms Heavy Infantry. Knight Heavy Infantry is an armored offensive core that follows through after a breach or shock action. Man-At-Arms Heavy Infantry is a disciplined line that holds, absorbs, and grinds.

### Strengths

- High first-contact impact
- Strong morale damage
- Better breach follow-through
- Better offensive pressure from elite heavy formations
- Strong prestige, honor, and title interactions after decisive victories

### Weaknesses

- Expensive upkeep and refit
- Punished hard by prepared pikes
- Poor at cheap skirmishing
- Poor at prolonged harassment
- Failed commitments should be painful

### Example Class Nodes

- `+1%` heavy cavalry charge impact
- `+1%` heavy infantry shock attack after breach
- `+1%` morale damage on first contact
- `+1%` cohesion while attacking through a breach
- `-1%` morale loss after honorable victory

### Example Tactical Unlocks

- `Lance Break`: Heavy Cavalry gains stronger first-contact morale damage, but suffers higher losses if countered by prepared pikes.
- `Noble Retinue`: Heavy Infantry gains offensive pressure after a wall or gate breach.
- `Oath Charge`: A committed charge is harder to cancel, but deals higher morale damage if it lands cleanly.

## Man-At-Arms

Man-At-Arms is the doctrine of disciplined professional soldiers.

This class is not glamorous in the same way as Knight, but it is the reason armies do not fall apart. It owns line discipline, pike walls, breach defense, and the brutal patience of holding ground.

### Formation Focus

- Heavy Infantry
- Light Infantry
- Pikemen

### Battlefield Identity

Man-At-Arms formations win by staying organized longer than the enemy.

Pikemen belong here because pikes are not about individual heroics. They require spacing, nerve, training, and discipline. A pike wall is the professional answer to a glorious cavalry charge.

### Strengths

- High cohesion under pressure
- Strong anti-cavalry defense
- Better defensive casualty control
- Better breach and gate holding
- Strong morale stability while holding position

### Weaknesses

- Lower pursuit
- Lower operational speed
- Less explosive than Knight
- Needs positioning and preparation
- Vulnerable to prolonged ranged pressure or siege without support

### Example Class Nodes

- `+1%` infantry cohesion
- `+1%` pike anti-cavalry impact
- `+1%` breach defense
- `-1%` morale loss while holding position
- `-1%` permanent-loss share while defending a breach

### Example Tactical Unlocks

- `Set Pikes`: Pikemen greatly reduce cavalry charge impact while stationary, but reposition more slowly.
- `Hold The Line`: Infantry gains cohesion and morale resistance while not pursuing.
- `Shielded Advance`: Light and Heavy Infantry reduce ranged losses while advancing slowly.

## Engineer

Engineers are the doctrine of siegecraft, fortification, and mechanical war.

They decide whether walls hold or fall. They are the only class that should directly improve breach math in a major way.

### Formation Focus

- Battering Ram
- Trebuchet
- Catapult
- Walls
- Traps
- Repairs
- Defensive siege

### Battlefield Identity

Engineers are slow, valuable, and strategically necessary.

An Engineer does not win by charging. An Engineer wins by changing the physical reality of the battle: opening a breach, bracing a gate, destroying enemy siege, laying traps, or buying the defender another hour.

### Strengths

- Best wall damage
- Best breach score generation
- Best counter-siege tools
- Better emergency repairs
- Better traps and obstacle systems
- Essential for serious city assaults

### Weaknesses

- Slow formations
- Needs escort
- Poor in open field if exposed
- Vulnerable to cavalry disruption
- Expensive losses when caught unsupported

### Example Class Nodes

- `+1%` wall damage
- `+1%` breach score generation
- `+1%` counter-siege accuracy
- `+1%` trap effectiveness
- `+1%` emergency repair rate

### Example Tactical Unlocks

- `Focused Bombardment`: Siege engines gain breach pressure against one wall section, but become easier to disrupt.
- `Counter-Battery Survey`: Defensive siege improves accuracy against enemy siege engines.
- `Rapid Bracing`: Engineers temporarily slow breach progress through emergency repairs.

## Ranger

Rangers are the doctrine of information, deception, terrain, and ranged fieldcraft.

They do not brute-force wars. They make the enemy wrong.

### Formation Focus

- Scouts/Rangers
- ShortBow
- LongBow
- CrossBow

### Battlefield Identity

Rangers are strongest before the battle becomes obvious.

They improve scouting, counter-scouting, ambushes, prepared ranged positions, false reports, ruins exploration, and early warning. A Ranger-heavy alliance should know more, hide more, and punish enemies who march blind.

### Strengths

- Better scout reliability and detail
- Better deception and false-report tools
- Better ambush setup
- Better ranged performance from prepared terrain
- Better warning for reinforcements, sally attempts, and Fighting Withdrawals

### Weaknesses

- Low wall pressure
- Weak in prolonged melee
- Vulnerable if cavalry reaches exposed ranged formations
- Needs information advantage to reach full value
- Poor at brute-force assaults

### Example Class Nodes

- `+1%` scout detection
- `+1%` scout stealth
- `+1%` deception strength
- `+1%` ranged accuracy from prepared terrain
- `-1%` scout capture chance during infiltration

### Example Tactical Unlocks

- `False Camp`: Ranger counter-scouts can distort enemy quick-scout reports.
- `Prepared Ambush`: Ranged formations gain first-volley impact if the enemy enters poorly scouted terrain.
- `Silent Watch`: Scouts provide earlier warning of incoming reinforcements, sally preparations, or Fighting Withdrawal attempts.

## Cavalry

Cavalry is the doctrine of movement, pursuit, harassment, and operational control.

It is not the same as Knight. Knights ask whether a charge can break the enemy. Cavalry asks whether the enemy can be caught, isolated, delayed, exhausted, or prevented from escaping.

### Formation Focus

- Ranged Cavalry
- Light Cavalry
- Heavy Cavalry

### Battlefield Identity

Cavalry controls the map around the battle.

It screens roads, intercepts reinforcements, harasses slow formations, catches routed units, supports Fighting Withdrawal pressure, and punishes exposed siege and ranged formations.

### Strengths

- Fastest operational movement
- Strong pursuit and rout conversion
- Better disengagement and repositioning
- Strong harassment against slow formations
- Strong interception against reinforcements and exposed support

### Weaknesses

- Poor wall pressure
- Weaker in breach fighting
- Vulnerable to pikes
- Sensitive to rough terrain
- Harassment creates fatigue if overused

### Example Class Nodes

- `+1%` march speed on open terrain
- `+1%` pursuit effectiveness
- `+1%` disengagement success
- `+1%` ranged cavalry harassment accuracy
- `-1%` fatigue from long mounted operations

### Example Tactical Unlocks

- `Harassing Circle`: Ranged Cavalry reduces enemy cohesion over time, but deals low direct casualties.
- `Cut Off Retreat`: Cavalry increases enemy permanent-loss and capture risk during routs.
- `Screen The Road`: Cavalry improves interception chances against reinforcements moving through nearby terrain.

## SellSword

SellSwords are the doctrine of mercenary economics and practical survival.

They are less about peak battlefield brilliance and more about staying useful, solvent, and dangerous through repeated wars.

### Formation Focus

- Mixed formations
- Replacements
- Recovery systems
- Contracts
- Ransom systems
- Campaign sustainment

### Battlefield Identity

SellSwords are the players who can keep fighting when everyone else is broke, wounded, or politically exhausted.

They are ideal for contract defense, opportunistic wars, ransom play, reduced upkeep, cheaper refits, and flexible reinforcement. They should be useful to alliances without becoming the strongest raw combat doctrine.

### Strengths

- Lower upkeep
- Cheaper training and refits
- Cheaper casualty replacement
- Better ransom prices
- Better recovery from failed attacks
- Better mixed-force morale recovery

### Weaknesses

- Weaker peak combat bonuses
- Less specialized in any one battlefield phase
- Struggles against specialists in ideal conditions
- Reputation may be politically mixed
- Economic power must be carefully capped

### Example Class Nodes

- `-1%` formation upkeep
- `-1%` refit cost
- `-1%` casualty replacement cost
- `-1%` ransom paid for captured scouts or formations
- `+1%` mixed-formation morale recovery

### Example Tactical Unlocks

- `Contract Guard`: Formations defending under a contract suffer reduced morale loss.
- `Hard Bargain`: Captured scouts and prisoners cost less to ransom back.
- `Paid To Survive`: Formations are slightly better at avoiding destruction after a failed attack, but gain less honor/title weight from heroic stands.

## Overlap Rules

Some formations belong naturally to more than one class identity.

This is intentional.

Overlap should create different fighting styles, not confusion.

Examples:

- Heavy Cavalry under Knight is shock and morale impact.
- Heavy Cavalry under Cavalry is mobility, interception, and pursuit.
- Heavy Infantry under Knight is offensive pressure and breach follow-through.
- Heavy Infantry under Man-At-Arms is discipline, cohesion, and holding power.
- CrossBow under Ranger is prepared ranged fieldcraft.
- CrossBow outside Ranger is still useful, but less tied to deception, terrain, and scouting support.

Class bonuses should be conditional enough that a formation feels different depending on doctrine.

## Alliance Design

A healthy alliance wants all six classes represented.

Suggested alliance functions:

- Knights create decisive breakthrough moments.
- Man-At-Arms hold lines, gates, and breaches.
- Engineers create or prevent breaches.
- Rangers provide intelligence, deception, and ranged preparation.
- Cavalry controls roads, pursuit, interception, and exposed support.
- SellSwords keep campaigns affordable and provide flexible contract muscle.

No class should solve every military problem alone.

This is what makes alliance warfare meaningful.

## Talent Tree Framework

Each player chooses one class tree at a time.

The tree should feel closer to a World of Warcraft-style specialization system than a small linear research branch.

Each class has `3` specialization choices.

Each specialization is a real build identity, not just a minor branch.

Baseline rules:

- Class level cap: `60`
- Talent point cap: around `60`
- Talent gain: `1 point per class level`
- Monthly class reset allowed
- Resetting class starts the new class tree from zero research progress
- Troop access is never locked by class
- Strong bonuses are conditional rather than global
- Each class has `3` specializations
- Each specialization should contain roughly `70` choices, ranks, or node decisions
- Players cannot take everything in a specialization
- Some nodes are shared between specializations in the same class
- Some nodes are shared across different classes when the military concept overlaps
- Specialty nodes define what makes that specialization unique

Recommended specialization tree structure:

- `Tier 1`: identity basics, available immediately
- `Tier 2`: requires `8-10` points in the specialization
- `Tier 3`: requires `18-22` points in the specialization
- `Tier 4`: requires `32-36` points in the specialization
- `Tier 5`: requires `45-50` points in the specialization
- `Capstone row`: requires `55+` points in the specialization

Recommended node caps:

| Node Type | Cap | Purpose |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Minor stat node | `2/2` or `3/3` | Small repeatable identity bonuses |
| Utility node | `2/2` or `3/3` | Cost, speed, warning, recovery, accuracy |
| Major tactic unlock | `1/1` | New tactical option or class behavior |
| Specialist node | `2/2` or `3/3` | Stronger bonus with narrow condition |
| Choice node | `1/1` each side | Mutually exclusive tactical style choice |
| Capstone | `1/1` | Class-defining late-tree identity |

Most percentage bonuses should remain small:

- Minor combat nodes: usually `+1%` per rank
- Utility/economy nodes: usually `1-2%` per rank
- Narrow specialist nodes: usually `2-3%` per rank if heavily conditional
- Capstones: strong behavior change, not a flat massive stat bonus

Design rule:

`Talent trees should make players better at their job, not better at every job.`

### Talent Count Target

Each specialization should be large enough that two players of the same class and same specialization can still differ.

Target per specialization:

| Category | Approx. Count |
| --- | ---: |
| Shared class nodes | `15-20` |
| Shared cross-class military nodes | `5-10` |
| Specialization-specific nodes | `35-45` |
| Choice nodes | `5-8` |
| Capstone choices | `2-3` |
| Total choices/ranks | Around `70` |

At max level, a player should be able to complete a build direction, but not the whole tree.

Expected max-level behavior:

- A focused player can reach one capstone route
- They can heavily invest in one side path
- They can take a few useful shared nodes
- They cannot take every defensive, offensive, utility, and economic tool at once

### Shared Nodes

Some talents should appear in multiple specializations or classes when the military idea is genuinely shared.

Examples:

- `Shielded Advance` can appear for Knight Retinue and Man-At-Arms Line, but with different follow-up nodes.
- `Set Pikes` belongs primarily to Man-At-Arms, but anti-cavalry awareness nodes may appear in Ranger or Engineer defensive trees.
- `Counter-Battery` belongs strongly to Engineer, but Ranger scouting can improve target identification.
- `Pursuit` appears in Cavalry, Knight, and SellSword, but each uses it differently.
- `Ransom` appears mainly in SellSword, but Ranger scout-capture and Knight execution/punishment choices can interact with it.

Shared nodes create familiarity. Specialty nodes create identity.

### Specialization List

Each class has three specializations:

| Class | Specialization 1 | Specialization 2 | Specialization 3 |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Knight | Shock | Retinue | Honor |
| Man-At-Arms | Line | Pike | Breach Defense |
| Engineer | Siegecraft | Fortification | Counter-Siege |
| Ranger | Scouting | Deception | Archery |
| Cavalry | Mobility | Harassment | Pursuit |
| SellSword | Contracts | Recovery | Ransom |

The sections below are seed designs for those specialization trees. They are not full `70`-choice trees yet. Each seed should eventually expand into a full specialization tree with shared nodes, choice nodes, and capstone routes.

### Full Specialization Tree Shape

Each full specialization tree should eventually use this approximate layout:

| Row | Unlock | Design Purpose | Approx. Choices |
| --- | --- | --- | ---: |
| 1 | `0` points | Basic class/spec identity | `4-6` |
| 2 | `5` points | Early utility and first combat direction | `6-8` |
| 3 | `10` points | First choice nodes and shared class tools | `7-9` |
| 4 | `18` points | Specialization mechanics begin to diverge | `8-10` |
| 5 | `25` points | Mid-tree tactical unlocks | `8-10` |
| 6 | `35` points | Strong conditional bonuses and counters | `8-10` |
| 7 | `45` points | Advanced identity and late utility | `7-9` |
| 8 | `55` points | Capstone row | `3-5` |

Total target:

`60-75 choices/ranks per specialization`

Tree pathing should include:

- Direct vertical routes for focused builds
- Side branches for utility
- Cross-links between related choices
- Mutually exclusive choice nodes
- Locked lower rows that require meaningful investment
- Capstones that reflect playstyle rather than raw universal strength

Example choice-node styles:

- `Commit` versus `Disengage`
- `Morale damage` versus `casualty damage`
- `Open-field strength` versus `siege-perimeter strength`
- `Reliable reports` versus `deceptive reports`
- `Cheaper recovery` versus `better ransom income`

The point is not to make every build mathematically equal in every situation.

The point is to make every build have a job, a cost, and a reason to exist.
